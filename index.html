When data voids lead to information voids

by EC, Rafiq Copeland, Jenny Fan and Tanay Jaeel

Monday, February 3rd was a tough day for the Iowa Democratic Caucus. A failure in a recently launched app that was supposed to report results meant that the voting outcomes were significantly delayed. While the US was waiting on results, a narrative started to merge that the faulty app was indicative of the Caucuses being rigged. This conspiracy theory was encouraged by President Trump and led to an increase in search activity about “Iowa Caucuses Rigged.” This, in turn, led to claims that conspiracy theorists were hijacking a narrative in the absence of high-quality, authoritative information which would erode people’s trust in democratic processes. 1

This begs the question: in today’s age of real-time reporting and optimization of authoritative content on search platforms, are people unable to access high-quality information?

Since their invention, search engines have transitioned from a convenient way to access indexed websites to an indispensable gateway to navigating the world wide web (Strategic Direction, 2014), with Google especially dominating 87% of all searches online (Statista, 2020). Whether users are looking up information (such as retrieving facts or answering questions) or exploring and discovering a new topic area, the field of information science acknowledges the disproportionate role of search engines in shaping a user's access to information online (Callender et al, 2010).
